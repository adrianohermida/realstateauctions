Real Estate Auctions: Rules and Procedures

Understand the rules and procedures for acquiring properties in judicial auctions.

The auction is one of the oldest known methods for the sale of assets. In this process, the asset is offered to a group of interested parties, and the highest bidder wins the purchase.

In the real estate sector, auctions offer a wide range of profitable opportunities in Brazil. Properties are often sold at values well below market price, making them highly attractive to investors. However, entering this market can be daunting due to its inherent complexities, including legal research, financial analysis, and regulatory procedures.

Types of Real Estate Auctions

Each auction modality has specific legal and operational characteristics:

  • Judicial Auctions: Conducted under court orders to resolve debts linked to legal actions. These auctions are backed by judicial authority and often offer properties at significantly reduced prices, making them ideal for strategic investments.
  • Extrajudicial Auctions: Initiated by financial institutions to recover properties from delinquent debtors. While the process tends to be quicker in terms of ownership transfer, prices may be closer to market value.
  • Public Tenders: Governed by public bidding rules, these auctions involve the sale of government-owned real estate. They are transparent and offer excellent long-term investment opportunities.

The Complexity of Auctions

Success in this market depends on a solid understanding of several essential steps:

  • Opportunity Research: Identifying viable investments requires market knowledge and reliable sources of information.
  • Property and Legal Analysis: Before bidding, one must evaluate the property's financial feasibility, structural condition, and any attached legal or tax liabilities.
  • Participation and Post-Auction Procedures: The auction process demands legal precision. Furthermore, the property's regularization often includes complex bureaucratic steps, such as debt clearance and registration updates.

Real Estate Auctions: Rules and Procedures

Understand the rules and procedures for acquiring properties in judicial auctions.

Who Can Bid

  • All legally competent individuals and duly registered legal entities may participate.
  • Individuals must present an official ID and taxpayer registration (CPF).
  • Companies must be represented by someone legally authorized, carrying the CNPJ and corporate documents.
  • All participants may appoint an attorney-in-fact with specific powers and proper identification.

Who Cannot Bid

  • Incompetent persons, the presiding judge, court staff, judicial officers, depositaries, or any relatives of these up to the second degree.
  • Appraisers and bailiffs who performed diligence in the case or administrators of the auctioned assets are also excluded.

Conditions of the Auction

  • Auctions are made in cash, to the highest bid.
  • Creditors may offer alternative payment options, such as installment plans.
  • For cash sales, at least 30% of the value must be deposited at the time of auction.
  • The balance must be paid within 15 days or the bidder will forfeit the deposit and be barred from future auctions.
  • For installment sales, the first installment must be paid at the auction event.
  • No withdrawal or post-auction complaints will be accepted.
  • If two bids are identical, preference goes to the bidder who previously won other auctions.
  • In Labor Court, full payment is required within 24 hours.

Additional Charges

  • 5% auctioneer’s fee (Law 6.830/80, art. 23).
  • Extra 5% if the item is a movable asset removed to the auctioneer’s deposit.
  • Judicial auction fee: 0.5% of the value, minimum R$ 10.64 and maximum R$ 1,915.38.
  • Storage and administrative fees (for private auctions).
  • All charges apply to the final bid value.
  • In Labor Court, the auctioneer’s fee is not charged; fees are the debtor’s responsibility.

Auction Process

  • The Auction Certificate or Delivery Writ will be issued 30 days after payment confirmation.
  • Delivery will be handled by a bailiff and may involve police support if needed.
  • After delivery, we handle all property regularization procedures, including judicial lien removal.
  • The average process time is 90 days, but may vary depending on the complexity.
  • For installment sales, the installment agreement must be submitted to receive the auction certificate.
  • If the auction is canceled, the full amount will be refunded with monetary correction.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is the origin of the auctioned properties? Properties are seized as collateral for debts in labor, civil, or federal cases.
  • Is the listed price final? No. It refers only to the opening bid. Other costs apply, including advisory fees and transfer expenses.
  • How does the auction work? Via public bidding or direct sale to the highest bidder.
  • What are public auctions? Scheduled court hearings for property sale. First auction: from appraised value; Second: highest reasonable bid.
  • What is a "low bid" (preço vil)? A bid below 50% (State Court) or 30% (Labor Court) of the appraised value may be rejected.
  • Can I know auction dates? We work exclusively with a single client per asset and do not disclose strategic case data.
  • Why are prices so low? Appraisals are depreciated to facilitate debt recovery, often at 50%-70% of market value.
  • Can I bid below appraised value? Yes. Bids with up to 70% discount may be accepted by the court, creditor, and debtor.
  • Can I visit the property? Not without judicial authorization. Visits must be approved and supervised by a bailiff.
  • Can I request authorization? Yes. It usually takes 30 days for the court to issue a visit authorization.
  • What if the property is in worse condition? If the property does not match the auction description, you may refuse delivery and request a refund.
